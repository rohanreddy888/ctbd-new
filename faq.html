Common Questions About Thalassemia

What is Thalassemia?

Thalassemia is a disorder of the blood in which a child is born with inability to form normal hemoglobin. This is so serious that unless the child receives blood transfusion by the age of 5 years of age

HOW DO WE RECOGNIZE THIS PROBLEM IN A CHILD?

This makes the child very weak and unable to do many activities that are normal for a healthy child. Activities that need physical exertion like active play are affected. He / she becomes irritable and cries at the smallest pretext. As the child grows even concentrating on academic activities are affected. The child’s tummy becomes protuberant as the spleen becomes abnormally big.

IS IT A NEW PROBLEM IN OUR COMMUNITIES?

Thalassemia is the most common single gene disorder across the world. It is more common among the people living in the Mediterranean region. The problem is not new to us in India but we certainly are becoming more aware as we have access to improving the quality of the lives of the affected children. It is seen more commonly among people.

It is seen more commonly among people from some regions and communities that include Sindhis, Punjabis, Gujaratis, Lohanas, kutchis, Agris, Mahars, Bengalis and Muslims as also among many groups in TS.

WHY SHOULD HEALTHY PEOPLE KNOW ABOUT THIS PROBLEM?

People who are Thalassemia carriers are otherwise healthy and lead normal active lives. The problem arises when two carriers have children. In a way this problem lies hidden until two carriers marry. As these healthy individuals carry the abnormal gene, they lead normal lives including becoming Parents. It is therefore important to become aware and reduce the risks to the next generations. Many come to know that they are carriers only after they have child with a major form of Thalassemia

WHAT OR.,WHO IS A THALASSEMIA CARRIER?

A person who carries one abnormal gene for Thalassemia does not suffer from the disorder. Such a person is a "carrier”... He/she can pass this on to his/her children who may just be carriers or suffer from Thalassemia major.

IT IS A PROBLEM TO BE CARRIER?

The problem of being a carrier is not to this individual. The problem instead will affect his/her children if the partner also is a carrier. When two carriers have children, the risk of a child with thalassemia major being born to them is very real. In every pregnancy that happens to such a couple the risk of a child with Thalassemia major being born is 25% and that is no small risk.

HOW MANY CARRIERS ARE GENERALLY THERE?

It is estimated that there are 40 million carriers among Indians. Approximately in 25 Indians is a carriers of Thalassemia gene.

HOW DO WE KNOW WHO IS CARRIER?

Almost all these carriers are normal and could get married to another carrier. The only way to identify a carrier is by testing.

CAN WE KNOW THE CARRIER STATUS FROM A ROUTING TEST

No it is not possible to diagnose a carrier through routine testing. On must ask for Thalassemia screening test to detect a carrier. Specific testing using techniques with great precision are required. Currently recommended method is called HPLC for separation of Hemoglobin types. It is advisable to have this testing done with a reputed laboratory physician.

HOW MUCH BLOOD WILL BE NEEDED TO DO THIS TEST BE NEEDED TO DO THIS TEST AND WHERE IS IT DONE?

A sample of 2ml blood is sufficient for testing for Thalassemia status.

HOW OFTEN MUST THE TESTING BE DONE?

This is a once in a lifetime test as the status of this inherited gene does not change during life.

CAN WE PREVENT THE BIRTH OF A THALASSEMIA MAJOR CHILD BY THIS TEST?

By detecting carriers it is possible to provide counseling and guidance. It is possible to know the status of the child as early as during the first 12 weeks of pregnancy.

HOW DO WE MAKE OUR FUTURE GENERATIONS THALASSEMIA FREE?

Screening for carrier status, avoiding marriage between carrier and asking for prenatal screening carrier and asking for prenatal screening are effective methods in the prevention of propagation of Thalassemia. These are already used as strategies that are effective in many countries.

MUST A THALASSEMIA CARRIER NOT HAVE A CHILD AT ALL?

A Thalassemia carrier can have children who would be either normal / carrier. It is important to know that the risk of Thalassemia major exists only when two carriers have a child.

WHAT ARE SOME SIMPLE WAYS TO PREVENT THE BIRTH OF A CHILD WITH THALASSEMIA MAJOR?

Knowing one’s carrier status before parenthood and screening the partner in case a carrier is detected will go a long way in preventing the birth of a child with Thalassemia major

WHAT CAN BE DONE IF TWO CARRIERS ARE DETECTED AFTER MARRIAGE?

Planning pregnancy and opting for prenatal diagnosis to establish the Thalassemia status of the child during pregnancy, as early as the first 12 weeks can provide useful information.

WHAT IS PRENATAL DIAGNOSIS AND HOW IS IT DONE?

This is a way to obtain a sample from the fetus during pregnancy. The sample obtained is used for testing for the presence of genetic disorders such as Thalassemia.

IS ANYTHING BEING DONE FOR THE THALASSEMIC CHILDREN AND CAN WE DO ANYTHING FOR THEIR WELFARE?

Across the world, in India and in TS there are NGOs providing safe blood transfusion facilities, guidance for handling complications due to the disease as well as frequent transfusion for the families affected is also part of their concern. In TS the Thalassemia and sickle cell society extends these facilities.

IF THIS IS SUCH A WELL KNOWN DISORDER WHY IS IT NOT COMMON KNOWLEDGE?

Like many issues in society, having a child with Thalassemia major is the first encounter with this disorder for most people.

WHO SHOULD BE SCREENED FOR THALASSAEMIA CARRIER STATUS?

Anyone who is a potential parent - which practically means all, are eligible for screening. Premarital screening is a wise step that can help avoid marriage between carriers. Screening of pregnant women gives another opportunity to detect before disaster strikes the family.

WHAT CAN WE DO TO HELP?

We could change the profile of the future generations with respect to Thalassemia by being part of awareness programmes, by getting ourselves and our children screened. We could advocate screening every time there is an opportunity we could donate blood that is life saving for the affected children. We could share this information with others.
